# Self-Employment Tax — 2026

## What Is Self-Employment Tax?

Self-employment tax is Social Security and Medicare tax for people who work for themselves. It is the equivalent of the FICA taxes that employers and employees split for W-2 workers.

Tom owes SE tax on net income from AiTheia LLC (if disregarded entity/sole prop) and any direct consulting income reported on 1099-NEC.

## 2026 Rates

| Component | Rate | Income Base |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| Social Security | 12.4% | First $168,600 of SE income |
| Medicare | 2.9% | All SE income (no cap) |
| Additional Medicare | 0.9% | SE income over $200K (single) / $250K (MFJ) |

**Total SE tax rate**: 15.3% (up to SS wage base) + 2.9% (above SS wage base) + 0.9% (above $200K/$250K)

## Calculation

1. Start with net self-employment income (Schedule C net profit or K-1 SE income)
2. Multiply by 92.35% (this accounts for the employer-equivalent portion)
3. Apply Social Security rate (12.4%) on first $168,600
4. Apply Medicare rate (2.9%) on all SE income
5. Apply Additional Medicare (0.9%) on amounts over threshold
6. Total = SE tax

**Deduction**: 50% of SE tax is deductible as an above-the-line deduction on Form 1040. This reduces AGI but not SE income.

## Example Calculation

If Tom has $100,000 net SE income:

```
Net SE income:                    $100,000
× 92.35%:                        $92,350

Social Security (12.4% × $92,350): $11,451
Medicare (2.9% × $92,350):         $2,678
Additional Medicare:                $0 (under threshold)

Total SE Tax:                     $14,129
Above-the-line deduction (50%):   $7,065
```

## W-2 Income Interaction

If Tom also has W-2 income from UCLA:
- UCLA already withholds FICA on W-2 wages
- The Social Security wage base ($168,600) is shared between W-2 and SE income
- If W-2 wages alone exceed $168,600, no additional SS tax on SE income
- Medicare tax (2.9%) still applies to all SE income regardless of W-2 wages
- Additional Medicare (0.9%) applies to combined W-2 + SE income over threshold

## S-Corp Advantage

If AiTheia elects S-Corp status:
- Tom pays himself a "reasonable salary" (W-2) — subject to FICA
- Remaining distributions are NOT subject to SE tax
- Potential savings on SE tax for distributions above reasonable salary
- Must document that salary is reasonable for services performed
- CPA should evaluate whether savings justify S-Corp administrative costs
